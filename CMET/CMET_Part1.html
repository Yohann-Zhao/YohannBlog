Atmospheric Composition

Primary Gases:

Nitrogen (N₂): 78% – Inert, stabilizes combustion.

Oxygen (O₂): 20% – Critical for combustion and respiration.

Trace Gases * :

Argon (0.9%), CO₂ (0.04%), ozone (O₃), water vapor (varies).

Ozone Layer

Location: Stratosphere (about 50 km altitude).

Role: Absorbs UV (Ultraviolet), protecting life and reducing cockpit UV exposure.

Thinning ("Ozone Hole") * :

Seasonal over Antarctica (Aug–Nov) → increased UV risk in southern Australia.

Equatorial Trough (Intertropical Convergence Zone, ITCZ)

Definition & Formation

A low-pressure belt near the equator where trade winds from the Northern and Southern Hemispheres converge.

Heated by direct solar radiation (sun overhead), causing warm air to rise, condense, and form clouds.

Climate Impact

Heavy Rainfall: Sustained convection → frequent thunderstorms, monsoons, and tropical weather.

Tropical Cyclogenesis: Favors cyclone formation when combined with warm ocean temperatures.

Seasonal Monsoons: Drives wet/dry seasons in tropical regions (e.g., Northern Australia in summer).

Seasonal Movement

Follows the Sun:

December (Southern Summer): Shifts south of the equator, bringing rain to Northern Australia.

June (Southern Winter): Moves north of the equator, drying Northern Australia.

Effect on Australia:

Summer: Monsoonal trough → flooding rains in Queensland/NT.

Winter: Dry season dominates.

Sub-Tropical Ridge (STR)

Definition & Formation

A high-pressure belt near 30°S latitude, formed by descending air from the Hadley Cell.

Descending Air: Compresses, warms, and inhibits cloud formation → stable, dry conditions.

Climate Impact

Trade Winds: Generates SE trade winds equatorward of the ridge.

Desert Formation * : Dominates arid zones (e.g., Australian Outback).

Seasonal Movement

Summer (Dec–Feb):

STR shifts south, closer to Antarctica.

Impact: Blocks cold fronts → stable, dry weather over Southern Australia.

Winter (Jun–Aug):

STR shifts north, toward the equator.

Impact: Allows cold fronts to penetrate Southern Australia → rainy winters.

Key To Remember About Pressure belts

Equatorial Trough: Drives tropical rainfall and monsoons; shifts with the sun.

Sub-Tropical Ridge: Governs arid climates and mid-latitude weather; seasonal north-south shifts.

Solar Radiation: Determines the strength and position of both systems, shaping Australia’s wet/dry seasons.

Role of Solar Radiation

1.    Sun’s Angle:

Direct overhead sun (summer solstice) heats the equatorial region intensely, deepening the Equatorial Trough.

Oblique sun (winter) reduces heating, weakening the trough.

2.    Pressure Gradient:

Strong solar heating → steeper pressure gradient → stronger trade winds.

Weak heating → flatter gradient → weaker winds (e.g., El Niño).
