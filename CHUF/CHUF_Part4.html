Part 4: The Human Eye

Eye Structure & Function

1.    Cornea

Structure: Transparent outer layer covering the iris and pupil.

Function: Refracts ~70% of light;

Susceptible to drying at high altitudes;

2.    Iris & Pupil

Structure: Colored muscular ring regulating pupil size.

Function: Adjusts light entry (pupil dilates in dim light, constricts in bright light).

3.    Lens

Structure: Flexible, transparent structure behind the iris.

Function: Accommodation: Changes shape via ciliary muscles to focus light on the retina.

Near Objects: Lens thickens.

Distant Objects: Lens flattens.

4.    Retina

Structure: Light-sensitive layer with photoreceptors (rods and cones).

Cones:

Located in fovea (central retina).

High acuity, color vision (red, green, blue).

Active in daylight.

Rods:

Peripheral retina.

Detect motion, low-light vision.

Insensitive to color/details.

Blind Spot: Optic nerve exit point; compensated by binocular vision.

Vision Types & Limitations

Binocular Vision

Definition: Overlapping visual fields from both eyes for depth perception (stereopsis).

Depth Perception: Effective up to 6 meters; beyond, relies on monocular cues (e.g., relative size).

Aviation Challenges:

Empty-Field Myopia: Eyes default to 1–2m focus in featureless environments (e.g., overcast skies).

Mitigation: Periodically focus on distant references (wingtips, clouds).

Double Vision: Caused by fatigue/disrupted eye coordination.

1.    Night Vision

Dark Adaptation:

Cones: Adapt in 5–7 minutes.

Rods: Require 30+ minutes for full sensitivity.

Hypoxia Impact: Night vision degrades above 4,000 ft; use supplemental O₂.

Protection:

Red cockpit lighting (preserves rod sensitivity).

Avoid white light (resets adaptation).

2.    Color Vision

Defects: 8% of males have red-green deficiency (difficulty reading color-coded instruments).

Visual Acuity & Defects

1.    Measurement

6/6 Vision: Normal (see at 6m what average person sees at 6m).

6/9 Vision: Can read at 6m what average person reads at 9m.

2.    Common Defects

Myopia (Nearsightedness): Image focuses before retina; corrected with concave lenses.

Hyperopia (Farsightedness): Image focuses behind retina; convex lenses.

Astigmatism: Irregular cornea/lens curvature; blurred vision at all distances.

Visual Scanning Techniques

1.    Day Scanning

Saccade-Fixation Cycle:

Eyes jump (saccades) every 10° with 0.3-sec pauses to focus.

Cover entire sky in sectors (prevents fixation).

Tip: Use head movements to avoid eye strain.

2.    Night Scanning

Off-Center Viewing: Focus 10–20° from target to use rod-dominated peripheral vision.

Collision Cues: Look for navigation lights (red/green/white) and relative motion against stars.

Visual Illusions & Mitigation

Tips about visual illusions on approach:

       Ground appear to be further à you feel higher à you want to fly lower, leads to undershoot

       Ground appear to be closer à you fell lower à you want to fly higher, leads to overshoot

1.    Common Illusions

Autokinesis: Stationary light appears to move in dark;

False Horizon: Misinterpreting cloud slopes/urban lights as horizon.

Black-Hole Approach: Runway with no surrounding lights, appears too high;

RWY aspect: Narrow runways, appears farther;

Upslope/downslop: upslope RWY, makes you feel higher.

2.    Environmental Factors

Haze: Distant objects appear farther;

Rain Refraction: Runway looks closer;

Protective Measures

Sunglasses: Polarized lenses with UV protection; remove in low light.

Oxygen Use: Mandatory above 10,000 ft (day) and 5,000 ft (night).

Instrument Cross-Check: Prioritize altimeter/glidepath over visual cues in poor visibility.
