Part 5: Disorientation

Some illusions are included in part 3 & part 4

Sense of Orientation

1.    Primary Senses:

Eyes (Vision): Provides ~90% of orientation information.

Vestibular System (Inner Ear): Detects angular/linear acceleration and head position.

Proprioceptive/Postural System: Senses body position and movement through muscles/joints.

2.    Priority:

Eyes > Vestibular > Proprioceptive.

In aviation, rely on instruments when visual cues are unreliable.

Disorientation

1.    Definition:

Confusion caused by conflicting sensory inputs (e.g., eyes vs. inner ear).

Common in low visibility (e.g., clouds, night) or featureless environments (e.g., over water).

2.    Factors Increasing Susceptibility:

Physical:

Head cold, sinus issues (affects inner ear).

Fatigue (reduces cognitive function).

Mental:

Anxiety, stress.

Distraction (e.g., multitasking).

Types of Disorientation

1.    Graveyard Spin/Spiral:

Cause: In clouds, pilot loses visual reference; inner ear fails to detect gradual turn.

Result:

Spin: Continuous rotation; pilot may feel level despite spinning.

Spiral: Increasingly tight turn; pilot may pull up, worsening the spiral.

Solution: Trust instruments (artificial horizon, turn coordinator).

2.    Autokinetic Illusion:

Cause: Viewing an isolated light in darkness (e.g., a star).

Result: Light appears to move; pilot may overcorrect.

Solution: Avoid fixating on a single light; scan instruments.

3.    Depth Perception Errors:

Binocular Vision: Effective only within ~1 meter.

Atmospheric Effects:

Haze/Fuzziness: Objects appear farther/higher.

Clear Air: Objects appear closer/lower.

Parallax:

Closer Objects: Move opposite to head movement.

Distant Objects: Move in the same direction as head movement.

Solutions to Disorientation

1.    Instrument Reliance:

Artificial Horizon: Primary reference for pitch and bank.

Turn Coordinator: Detects yaw and roll.

Altimeter, Airspeed Indicator: Cross-check for altitude/speed control.

2.    Visual Priority:

Ignore conflicting vestibular/proprioceptive signals.

Use external visual references (e.g., horizon, runway) when available.

3.    Preventive Measures:

Training: Practice instrument flying and recovery techniques.

Health: Treat sinus issues; avoid flying fatigued.

Focus: Minimize distractions; prioritize situational awareness.

Spatial Disorientation and Illusions

Illusion

Cause

Effect

Solution/Prevention

Somatogravic Illusion

Linear acceleration (e.g., takeoff).

False sensation of pitching up.

Rely on instruments; avoid over-reliance on vestibular cues.

The Leans

Fluid in semicircular canals lags during gradual roll; sudden correction.

False sensation of banking in the opposite direction.

Trust instruments; avoid sudden head movements.

Coriolis Illusion

Head movement in multiple planes (e.g., pitch + roll).

Severe disorientation; yaw misinterpreted as roll.

Minimize head movements; rely on instruments.

Graveyard Spin/Spiral

Loss of visual reference in clouds; inner ear fails to detect gradual turn.

Spin: Continuous rotation; Spiral: Tightening turn, potential stall.

Trust instruments (artificial horizon, turn coordinator); avoid overcorrecting.

Prevention of Illusions

Reliance on sensory input instead of instruments.

Disorientation, incorrect control inputs.

Rely on flight instruments; ignore conflicting sensory input; maintain situational awareness.
