Liveware (Human Factors)

Ergonomic Principle: Fit the people, prioritizing intuitive use, comfort, and safety.

Cockpit Interfaces

Interfaces bridge pilots and aircraft systems through controls (inputs) and displays (outputs).

Natural Sense: Intuitive design aligning with human instincts (e.g., throttle forward = increase thrust).

Balanced Design: Prioritize by:

Importance: Critical functions (e.g., attitude indicator) placed centrally.

Frequency: Commonly used controls (e.g., trim) within easy reach.

Effort: Minimize physical strain (e.g., touchscreens reduce manual input).

Feedback: Confirm actions (e.g., auditory click for button presses, visual confirmation on screens).

Safety Equipment

Safety Harnesses

Single Sash Harness:

Effective during linear deceleration.

Drawback: Side loads can cause the upper body to lean.

Double Shoulder Strap:

Provides restraint for both linear and sideways deceleration.

Inertia Reels

Designed to lock when subjected to approximately 1 G, helping secure the seatbelt during abrupt movements.

Ergonomic Positioning

Design Eye Position (DEP)

Based on the 5th to 95th percentile range of the population.

Ensures that critical displays are positioned for the middle 90% of users, accepting that the extreme 5% at each end might not be optimally served.

Bottleneck in Perception

Observation:

The critical transition from sensation to perception is a bottleneck in information processing.

Displays and Instrumentation

Bottleneck: Sensory data (e.g., instrument readings) must be perceived and interpreted.

Example: A flashing warning light (sensation) requires the pilot to recognize its meaning (perception).

Display Types:

Digital: Precise values (e.g., altitude readout) but slower to interpret.

Analog: Quick trend recognition (e.g., climb rate via vertical speed indicator).

Head-Up Displays (HUD): Project data onto the windshield, maintaining external focus.

Warnings: Audible alerts prompt immediate action; overly frequent alarms risk desensitization.

Linear Strip/Tape Displays: Vertical/horizontal scales (e.g., altimeter tape) may create illusory motion (e.g., "rolling" effect during climbs).

Glass Cockpit Challenges:

Advantages: Consolidates information on a few simple screens (e.g., PFD, MFD).

Drawbacks: May result in a loss of overall situational awareness.

Can erode basic instrument scanning skills.

Risk of mode confusion with autopilot systems and potential errors.

Communication in the Cockpit

Verbal exchanges remain crucial in the cockpit.

Communication Barriers

External Factors:

Environmental noise, distractions, physical discomfort.

Variations in enunciation and accents.

Internal Factors:

Misinterpretation or selective perception.

Common pitfalls include:

Expectancy bias (hearing what is expected).

Partial listening (missing portions of the conversation).

Limitations inherent to language syntax.

Communication Aids and Techniques

Standard Phrases:

Use ICAO terminology (e.g., "Roger," "Wilco") to reduce ambiguity.

Controlled Pace:

Maintain clear and deliberate speech.

Two-Way Communication Rule:

Always wait for a response from each crew member before moving on.

If there is no response, repeat the message.

Authority Gradient

Shallow Gradient:

Promotes equal involvement but may dilute the command’s authority.

Steep Gradient:

Risks creating a “dictator” environment where subordinates may hesitate to communicate.

Cultural Considerations:

Some cultures (e.g., in parts of Asia and Europe) traditionally maintain a steeper authority gradient.

Nonverbal Communication

Key Elements:

Eye Contact:

Brief and direct eye contact is effective; prolonged staring may be perceived as threatening.

Facial Expression and Gestures:

Convey confidence and clarity.

Touch:

Generally avoided beyond initial handshakes.

Push & Pull Communication

Push Mode:

Involves expressing information assertively.

Pull Mode:

Involves actively seeking or clarifying information.

Active Listening

Importance:

Essential for effective two-way communication.

Challenges:

Wandering attention.

Prematurely preparing responses.

Disinterest or diversion during conversations.

Impact:

Up to 75% of communicated information can be lost if active listening is not maintained.

Leadership and Followership

Leadership

Distinction:

Authority: Assigned by position.

Leadership: Developed through skills and experience.

Three-Circle Model of Good Leadership:

Task: Managing operations.

Team: Building and supporting crew dynamics.

Individual: Recognizing and nurturing personal development.

Effective Leadership Practices (P+G+ type):

Lead by example.

Clearly advise your intent.

Delegate responsibilities appropriately.

Motivate and consult with crew members.

Set clear priorities.

Followership

Key Considerations:

Team Cohesion:

Put personality differences aside to support a unified approach.

Assertiveness:

Correct and Safe Plan:

Full support is essential.

Incorrect or Dangerous Plan:

It is critical to voice strong objections.

Threat and Error Management (TEM) Overview

Components of TEM:
TEM organizes operational factors into three basic components:

1.   Threats: External or inherent factors that increase operational complexity.

2.   Errors: Deviations in crew performance—whether spontaneous or induced—that, if uncorrected, can lead to undesired states.

3.   Undesired Aircraft States (UAS): Conditions where the aircraft deviates from its intended state (e.g., position, speed, configuration), serving as a potential precursor to adverse outcomes.

Crew Actions & Countermeasures

Role of the Flight Crew:
Crew interventions are critical at every stage of the TEM process. Approximately 70% of flight crew activities involve implementing countermeasures to manage threats, errors, or UAS. The crew may act to:

Anticipate and Counter Threats: Through pre-flight planning, weather analysis, and system familiarization.

Recognize and Prevent Errors: By vigilant monitoring, cross-checking instruments, and adhering to Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs).

Correct UAS: By promptly restoring controlled, stabilized flight.

TEM Stages and Corresponding Countermeasures:

1.   Threat Stage:

Actions: Anticipation and implementation of preventive measures.

Tools: Pre-flight inspections, weather briefings, and operational planning.

2.   Error Stage:

Actions: Early recognition and prevention of deviations.

Tools: Checklists, cross-checking, and strict adherence to SOPs.

3.   UAS Stage:

Actions: Immediate correction to return to normal flight conditions.

Tools: Tactical recovery procedures and crew resource management (CRM).

Threats
Threats are events or conditions, including errors made by others, that occur outside the immediate control of the flight crew and increase operational complexity.

Types of Threats:

1.   External Threats:
Originate from the environment and operational context, such as:

Weather conditions

Air Traffic Services (ATS) issues

Airport environment and terrain

Operational pressures

Aircraft malfunctions

2.   Internal Threats:
Stem from factors inherent to the crew or operation, including:

Pilot fatigue, health, and fitness

Team familiarity and dynamics

Language, cultural differences, and personality

Experience levels

Temporal Threat Categories:

1.   Expected Threats:
Can be anticipated during flight planning.

2.   Unexpected Threats:
Arise suddenly, such as in-flight malfunctions.

3.   Latent Threats:
Hidden issues (e.g., design flaws, scheduling pressures, or systemic inadequacies) that may not be immediately obvious.

Organizational Threats:
Deficiencies in the infrastructure of flight operations (e.g., maintenance, ground handling, dispatch, documentation, and crew scheduling) that indirectly affect flight safety.

Errors
Errors are deviations—through action or inaction—by the flight crew that can compromise safety by leading to UAS. Their impact depends on whether they are detected and corrected before causing a critical event.

Categories of Errors:

1.   Aircraft Handling Errors:

Manual Flight or Autopilot Management: Involving data entry, throttle settings, etc.

Subtypes:

Systematic Errors: Consistent and predictable, allowing for easier identification and correction.

Random Errors: Unpredictable

Sporadic Errors: Occasional yet severe, and generally the hardest to remedy.

2.   Procedural Errors:

Deviations from SOPs and checklists (e.g., omitted steps, incorrect callouts, misinterpretation of aircraft limitations).

3.   Communication Errors:

Use of non-standard phraseology, poor radio quality, over-transmission, and challenges such as unfamiliar accents or rapid speech.

Human Error Considerations:

Human error is inevitable and is a factor in roughly 80% of all accidents.

Experience can reduce handling errors, yet judgment errors often persist regardless of experience.

Vigilance decrement: Human attention tends to decline after the first 30 minutes of operation, increasing the likelihood of missed cues—even with automation like the autopilot.

Undesired Aircraft States (UAS)
UAS are flight conditions where the aircraft deviates from its intended state—such as in altitude, speed, configuration, or position—which can lead to further errors or accidents.

1.   Aircraft Handling States:

Deviations in altitude (ALT), manual handling (MH), and airspeed (AS).

Non-stabilized approaches, off-centerline touchdowns, or penetration into restricted airspace.

2.   Ground Navigation States:

Taxiing for wrong RWY/TWY, excess taxiing speed, positioned on wrong TWY/Ramp/Gate/Holding Point etc.

3.   Aircraft Configuration States:

Incorrect aircraft or systems configuration (e.g., autopilot mode errors, wrong NAVAID, improper power or fuel management).

 

TEM Management Strategies

Threat Management:

Involves both anticipation and response to threats through tools such as pre-flight inspections, weather studies, and comprehensive briefings.

Managed threats are those identified and countered before they impact safety, whereas mismanaged threats may trigger a cascade of errors.

Error Management:

Emphasizes early error detection and correction to prevent UAS.

Involves systematic countermeasures (e.g., built-in alerts like GPWS, ACAS) and active crew monitoring.

Transition from Error to UAS Management:

Recognizing when an error has escalated to a UAS is critical.

Once in a UAS, the primary focus shifts to immediate recovery and stabilization to avoid incident escalation.

Countermeasures Overview:

Systemic-Based Countermeasures:
Include hard resources like stall warnings, systems failure alerts, and standardized procedures (SOPs, checklists, briefings).

Individual and Team Countermeasures:

1.    Planning Countermeasures (Before Execution)

Pre-flight preparation: Comprehensive planning, including weather assessment, operational briefings, and aircraft checks.

Briefing: Setting

Contingency planning: Anticipating potential threats and pre-determining response strategies.

2.    Execution Countermeasures (During Flight Operations)

System monitoring: Actively observing aircraft systems and automation.

Cross-checking: Real-time verification of actions (not after execution, but while performing them).

Workload management: Efficiently allocating tasks between crew members.

Forward thinking: Continuously considering the next step in the operation.

3.    Review Countermeasures (Post-Execution and Ongoing)

Progress review: Assessing whether current actions align with operational goals.

Plan modification: Adjusting strategies based on new information or situational changes.

Maintaining assertiveness: Staying engaged by continuously reviewing progress and anticipating future events.

 

Execution vs. Review Countermeasures

Execution Countermeasures:

Applied while performing a task (e.g., actively cross-checking instruments, workload distribution, thinking ahead to the next step).

Review Countermeasures:

Applied after execution to evaluate effectiveness and adapt plans (e.g., modifying approach strategies, remaining assertive by reviewing past progress, staying alert to future developments).
