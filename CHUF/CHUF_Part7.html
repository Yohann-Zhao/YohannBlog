Part 7: Circulation, Respiration

Hypoxia

1.   Body Reflex for Breathing

Normal Reflex:

The brain monitors CO₂ levels to regulate breathing.

At Altitude (ALT):

No immediate reflex to increase breathing rate despite lower oxygen levels.

Oxygen Level Maintenance:

Pressurized Cabin: Maintains oxygen levels at safe altitudes.

Oxygen Mask: Provides supplemental oxygen when needed.

Wrong Method:

Breathing quickly decreases CO₂ levels but does not increase oxygen levels.

2.   Effects of Hypoxia

Subtle Deception:

Mild Hypoxia:

Similar to alcohol intoxication.

Euphoria and decreased performance.

Severe Hypoxia:

Erratic or bizarre behavior.

Loss of consciousness (LOC).

No feeling of suffocation.

Cyanosis (bluish discoloration of the skin).

3.   Factors Increasing Susceptibility to Hypoxia

Smoking:

Smoker’s Lungs (S.L.): Effective altitude starts at 8,000 ft.

At 5,000 ft, smokers feel effects equivalent to 10,000 ft.

Alcohol: Reduces oxygen-carrying capacity.

Cold Temperature and Fatigue: Increase metabolic demand for oxygen.

Chronic Respiratory Diseases: Asthma, etc., impair oxygen uptake.

Heart Disease: Reduces oxygen delivery to tissues.

Anemia: Low hemoglobin reduces oxygen-carrying capacity.

Hyperventilation

Oxygen Availability:

Depends on partial pressure, not volume.

Breathing Effect:

Decreases CO₂ levels but does not increase oxygen levels.

Symptoms:

Tingling in hands and feet.

Light-headedness.

Remedy: reduce breathing rate

Effects of Reduced Pressure

1.   Gas Expansion

Abdomen and Toothache:

Trapped gas expands, causing discomfort.

Descend to relieve symptoms.

2.   The Bends (Decompression Sickness)

Cause:

Nitrogen comes out of solution in body tissues.

Normal Conditions: Occurs above 18,000 ft but is rare below 25,000 ft.

After Scuba Diving: Can occur as low as 8,000 ft.

Symptoms:

Joint pain and stiffness.

Muscle pain.

Tingling sensation or paralysis.

3.   Rapid Decompression

Scenario:

Failure of a door or window at high altitude.

Effects:

Breath is forcibly exhaled from the lungs.

Pain in the stomach, ears, and sinuses.

Differentiating symptoms of Hyperventilation, Hypoxia, CO Poisoning, and The Bends

Hyperventilation

Rapid breathing → ↓ CO₂ levels

Symptoms mimic hypoxia but caused by low CO₂ (not low O₂). No cyanosis.

Hypoxia

Euphoria, impaired judgment, cyanosis (blue skin), unconsciousness, no suffocation

Bright red skin (cherry-red lips), no cyanosis. Symptoms worsen with prolonged exposure.

Altitude-Related Numbers to Remember

1.   Atmospheric Pressure and Oxygen Levels

Total Atmospheric Pressure: Halves at 18,000 feet.

Partial Pressure of Oxygen (O₂): Halves at 9,000 feet.

2.   Effects on Smokers

At Sea Level (SL), a smoker’s blood oxygen level is equivalent to a non-smoker’s at 8,000 feet.

At 5,000 feet, a smoker’s blood oxygen level is equivalent to a non-smoker’s at 10,000 feet.

3.   Night Vision Impairment:

Normal people: Impaired above 4,000 feet due to hypoxia.

Smokers: Impaired even at Sea Level.

4.   Supplemental Oxygen Requirements

For Pilots:

Sea Level – 10,000 feet

Not required.

10,000 – 25,000 feet

Continuous flow oxygen.

25,000 – 40,000 feet

Demand oxygen mask (oxygen percentage starts increasing).

At 33,700 feet

Oxygen percentage increases to 100%.

Above 40,000 feet

Pressure demand mask (100% oxygen under pressure).

For Passengers: Supplemental oxygen required above 14,000 feet.
