Part 8: Decision Making, Personality, Attitudes

Evolution of Aviation Accidents

Historical Shift in Causes:

Past: Accidents were often attributed to poor engineering.

Present: Modern incidents are more frequently linked to inadequate performance and poor judgment.

Human Error: Currently, human error is estimated to account for about 80% of all aviation accidents.

Actions and Decision Making

Types of Actions:

Subconscious Actions: Automatic, reflex-driven responses that occur without deliberate thought.

Deliberate Actions: Conscious, calculated responses based on situational analysis and decision making.

Information Processing Cycle:

1.   Sensation: Reception of raw data from the environment.

2.   Perception: Interpretation and organization of sensory input.

3.   Decision: Evaluating the options based on the interpreted data.

4.   Response: Executing the chosen action.

Mental Capacity: Human processing power is finite. Increasing the cognitive demand in one area may reduce available capacity for other tasks.

Situational Awareness

Effective situational awareness in aviation is maintained through multiple sensory inputs:

Visual: Observing instruments and the external environment.

Audio: Responding to alerts and ambient sounds.

Vestibular: Maintaining balance and spatial orientation.

Proprioceptive/Postural: Understanding body position and movement.

Memory Systems in the Cockpit

Sensory Memory

Role: Provides immediate, short-lived storage of sensory data.

Functions: Initiates reflexes, transfers information to short-term memory, then discards non-essential data.

Duration by Type:

Visual: Approximately 1 second.

Audio: Approximately 5 seconds.

Short-Term Memory (Working Memory)

Capacity: Can hold about 7

Nature: Primarily acoustic; nearly error-free when information is simple.

Enhancement Techniques:

Chunking/Grouping: Organizing information into meaningful units.

Rehearsal and Association: Linking new information to long-term memory stores.

Long-Term Memory

Characteristics: More extensive but susceptible to error due to complex associations.

Types:

Semantic Memory: Knowledge about facts and general information.

Episodic Memory: Personal experiences and specific events.

Motor Skills Memory

Development: Transition from individual actions through repeated practice to the formation of refined motor programs.

Outcome: Improved execution of tasks and smoother performance under operational conditions.

Stress, Attention, and Arousal

Impact of Stress:

Increases the rate of incoming information while simultaneously decreasing the span of effective processing.

Attention Under Stress:

Normal Conditions: Can handle both sequential and divided attention.

Under Stress: Attention narrows to selective sequential processing—focusing on the most critical elements.

Performance vs. Arousal Curve:

Demonstrates that optimal performance is achieved at moderate arousal levels, whereas too little or too much arousal can impair performance.

 

Cockpit Design Considerations

Objective: To preserve spare mental capacity and ensure effective decision making.

Design Strategies:

Divided Sequential Attention: Aids in efficiently scanning instruments.

Selective Sequential Attention: Helps manage critical tasks during periods of high stress.

Personality Factors

Stable Personality Traits

Example: Extroversion, which may contribute to effective communication and teamwork.

Positive Attributes:

P+ (Proactive): Strong initiative.

G+ (Goal-Oriented): Clear focus on achieving targets.

Negative Attributes:

G- (Compromising on Goals): Tendency to settle, potentially lowering performance.

P- (Lacking Empathy): May lead to poor interpersonal interactions.

Hazardous Personality Traits

Anti-Authority: Resistance to following established protocols.

Over-Deference: Excessive compliance, which may prevent necessary challenges to unsafe decisions (e.g., “I can’t stop what the captain wants to do”).

Impulsiveness: Making hasty decisions without full evaluation.

Invulnerability: Overconfidence with the belief that “everything will always be OK.”

Macho: Risk-taking behavior that can lead to unsafe outcomes.

Resignation: A tendency to give up under pressure, impairing effective decision making.

Attitude and Motivation

Attitude Formation

Components:

Belief (Cognitive): What one perceives as true.

Feeling (Affective): The emotional response to beliefs.

Action (Behavioral): The behavior that results from these beliefs and feelings.

Sequence: Belief → Feeling → Action, collectively shaping one’s overall attitude.

Motivation

Types:

Extrinsic Motivation: Driven by external rewards (e.g., salary, recognition, job security).

Intrinsic Motivation: Driven by internal satisfaction (e.g., passion for flying, personal fulfillment).

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs in Aviation

Basic Needs: Physiological requirements and safety/security are paramount.

Social Needs: Belonging and affection within the team foster cooperation.

Esteem and Self-Actualization: Recognition, self-esteem, and opportunities for personal growth are key motivating factors.

Practical Application:

Motivators: Responsibility, the nature of the job, and advancement opportunities.

Hygiene Factors: Adequate staffing, competitive salary, and overall security to prevent dissatisfaction.
