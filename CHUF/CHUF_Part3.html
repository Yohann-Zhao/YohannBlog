Part 3: The Human Ear

Structure and Function

The ear is divided into three main sections, each

1. Outer Ear

Function: Collects and directs sound waves into the ear.

Components:

Pinna (Auricle): The visible part of the ear that funnels sound waves into the auditory canal.

Auditory Canal: A tube-like structure that carries sound waves to the eardrum (tympanic membrane).

Eardrum: Vibrates in response to sound waves, transmitting them to the middle

2. Middle Ear

Function: Amplifies and transmits sound vibrations to the inner ear.

Components:

Ossicles: Three tiny bones (malleus, incus, and stapes) that amplify and transfer vibrations from the eardrum to the inner ear.

Eustachian Tube: Connects the middle ear to the throat and helps equalize pressure.

Blockage: Can cause discomfort, pain, or hearing impairment due to trapped pressure (e.g., during altitude changes in aviation).

Swallowing, yawning, or the Valsalva maneuver can help equalize pressure.

3. Inner Ear

Function: Converts sound vibrations into neural signals and maintains balance.

Components:

Cochlea: A spiral-shaped organ responsible for auditory processing. It contains hair cells that convert sound vibrations into electrical impulses sent to the brain.

Vestibular System: Responsible for balance and spatial orientation.

Otolith Organs:

Utricle: Detects horizontal linear acceleration.

Saccule: Detects vertical linear acceleration.

Semicircular Canals: Detect angular acceleration (rotational movements).

Balance and Sensory Integration

Balance relies on input from three sensory systems:

1.    Visual (Eyes): Provides information about the body’s position relative to the environment.

2.    Vestibular System (Inner Ear): Detects motion and orientation.

3.    Proprioceptive System (Muscles and Joints): Provides feedback on body position and movement.

Sensory Conflict: Discrepancies between these systems can lead to spatial disorientation or vertigo, especially in aviation environments with limited visual cues.

Spatial Disorientation and Illusions

1.   Somatogravic Illusion: Caused by linear acceleration (e.g., during takeoff), leading to a false sensation of pitching up.

2.   The leans: When body rotate, the fluid in the semicircular canal stays stationary, cause sensory hairs move to the opposite direction of rotation.

The fluid gradually stabilizes in a gradual, slow roll of the aircraft. When the pilot finally corrects the roll, the sudden movement is detected by the vestibular system, creating a false sensation of banking in the opposite direction.

3.   Coriolis Illusion: Occurs during simultaneous head movements in multiple planes, causing severe disorientation.

There are 3 canals. If you pitch your head down, the one usually used for sensing roll will now being put in the plane at which yaw happens, and your vestibular sense will take yaw movements as roll.

Prevention of illusions: Rely on flight instruments, ignore sensory input.

Noise and Hearing Protection

Frequency (Hz): Determines the pitch of sound.

Intensity (dB): Measures the loudness of sound.

Comfort Level: Below 80 dB.

Hazardous Levels: Regular exposure above 85 dB can cause permanent hearing loss.

Pain Threshold: Around 140 dB.

Hearing Protection:

Earplugs: Reduce noise by approximately 25 dB.

Headsets: Provide up to 40 dB of noise reduction and are essential in high-noise environments like cockpits.

Effects of Alcohol on the Ear

Viscosity Changes: Alcohol alters the density of the fluid in the inner ear, affecting balance and coordination

Hearing Impact: Low-frequency hearing loss is often the first noticeable effect.

Aviation Implications: Alcohol consumption can impair spatial orientation and increase the risk of spatial disorientation, even hours after consumption.
