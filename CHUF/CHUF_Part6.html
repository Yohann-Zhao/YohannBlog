Part 6: G Load & Toxic Hazards

Effects of G Forces

1.   Positive G Forces

Cause:

Blood is pulled away from the brain toward the lower body.

Internal organs are stretched, and arteries are elongated.

Trigger Reflex:

Brain detects reduced blood flow and triggers a reflex.

Seconds later, the heart increases its pumping rate to compensate.

Effects:

Transient G Loads: Residual oxygen in the brain sustains consciousness briefly.

Sustained G Loads:

Grey Out (3.5G): Vision begins to fade due to reduced blood flow to the eyes.

Black Out (5G): Complete loss of vision due to insufficient blood flow to the brain.

G-LOC (G-Induced Loss of Consciousness) (>5G): Loss of consciousness due to critical lack of oxygen to the brain.

2.   Negative G Forces

Cause:

Excess blood flows toward the brain.

Trigger Reflex:

Heart rate slows down to reduce blood flow to the brain.

Effects:

Red Out:

Blood pools in the head, causing the bottom eyelid to rise over the eyeball.

Symptoms include bloodshot eyes, red face, and discomfort.

Safe Limit: -3G (beyond this, risk of injury increases).

 

Factors That Decrease G Tolerance

Health Conditions:

Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar).

Diabetes (reduced blood volume).

Anemia (low hemoglobin levels).

Environmental Factors:

Heat stress (increases dehydration and reduces blood volume).

Illnesses:

Respiratory infections or colds (reduce oxygen intake and overall tolerance).

Transition from Negative to Positive G:

Slowed heart rate from negative G reduces tolerance to subsequent positive G forces.

Techniques to Improve G Tolerance

1.    Reclined Seating:

Reduces the vertical distance between the heart and brain, improving blood flow.

2.    Muscle Tensing:

Tightening stomach and leg muscles to prevent blood pooling in the lower body.

3.    Anti-G Suits (G-Suits):

Inflatable bladders in the suit compress the lower body, preventing blood from pooling.

4.    Physical Fitness:

Regular exercise improves cardiovascular health and overall G tolerance.

Carbon Monoxide (CO) Symptoms

CO poisoning can have varying effects depending on the concentration and exposure duration:

Very Low Levels:

No noticeable symptoms.

Rising Levels:

Insidious Performance Decrease: Subtle decline in cognitive and physical performance.

First Obvious Symptoms:

Slight headache.

Fatigue.

Mild breathing discomfort.

High Levels:

Severe headache.

Nausea and vomiting.

Mental confusion and impaired vision.

Loss of consciousness (LOC).

Death (if exposure continues).

Cabin Heat Exchanger

The cabin heat exchanger regulates air temperature by transferring heat between cabin air and exhaust gases.

Outer Section:

Cabin Air Intake: Cold air enters, and hot air leaves after heating.

Inner Section:

Hot Exhaust Gases: Enter and leave after transferring heat.

 

CO Leakage Risk:

If the inner section leaks, CO can mix with cabin air, posing a serious health hazard.

Actions if CO Leakage is Suspected:

1.    Turn Off Cabin Heat: Prevent further CO entry.

2.    Increase Fresh Air Supply: Ventilate the cabin.

3.    Use Oxygen Masks: Provide supplemental oxygen to occupants.

Toxic Substances in General Aviation

Toxic substances can be encountered in various aviation operations and pose health risks.

Common Toxic Substances:

Fuel additives.

Cleaning agents.

Aerial agriculture sprays (e.g., pesticides).

Defoliants.

Compressed gases and fire extinguishing agents.

Symptoms of Exposure:

Skin irritation.

Headache.

Nausea.

Precautions and Protections:

Avoid Shortcuts: Follow proper procedures at all times.

Do Not Handle if in Doubt: Seek guidance or avoid contact with unknown substances.
