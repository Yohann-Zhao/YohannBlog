Cardiovascular Health

Heart Disease: Primarily genetic; secondary factors include diet, exercise, and smoking.

Hypertension: Linked to obesity, high salt intake.

Age-Related Changes

Hearing Loss: High-frequency deterioration.

Presbyopia: Long-sightedness (correct with convex glasses).

Obesity & Related Diseases

Risks: Hypertension, diabetes, osteoarthritis (over worn joints), gout (high uric acid + poor diet).

Smoking:

Carbon monoxide: occupy hemoglobin easier than oxygen → lead to hypoxia

Impact: impairs night vision at sea level(smoker: like normal people at 8,000 ft), reduces mental alertness.

Environmental Factors

Cabin Conditions

Optimal Temperature: 21–27°C.

Humidity: Ideal 40–60% (aircraft cabins often <10% → dehydration risk).

Thermal Stress

Heat Stress (Temp >32°C or <10°C):

[common misunderstanding: heat stress is being too hot]

Symptoms: Fatigue, loss of concentration.

Prevention: Hydrate; avoid alcohol (impairs thermoregulation).

Hypothermia: Prolonged cold exposure → impaired cognition, unconsciousness.

Don’t use Alcohol: False sense of warmth, reduces resistance

Dehydration

Loss: 1 L/day (up to 5 L in heat).

Prevention: Drink water beyond thirst (water, juices OK).

Medical Risks in Flight& Precautions

Common Pilot Incapacitation:

No.1 Cause: Gastrointestinal upsets (food poisoning).

Avoid: Reheated/prepared foods, seafood (especially shellfish).

Safe: Freshly opened canned food, no gas-producing vegetables (avoid greens like broccoli).

Gas Expansion:

Climb: Gastrointestinal gas pain

Avoid gas-producing foods: green vegetables.

Descent: Ear/sinus pressure

Barotrauma: Eustachian Tube Blockage (most pain during descent):

Symptoms: Pain, pressure vertigo.

Remedy: Valsalva maneuver (pinch nose, blow gently).

Sinus Issues: Colds/allergies → blocked vents → pain.

Avoid Flying: If you caught colds and congested.

Medication:

DAME: Designated Aviation Medical Examiner

Injuries/Illness: Clearance required by DAME before flying.

Medication/Medicine: Always consult DAME.

Hay Fever: Antihistamines cause drowsiness; consult DAME.

Pregnancy: After 6 months’ pregnancy, recommend don’t fly (limited cockpit mobility).

Blood Donation: recommend 24-hour grounding after donation (due to lowered blood pressure).

Countermeasures: Airsickness VS illusions

Airsickness & Remedies

Symptoms: Nausea, sweating.

Mitigation: Focus on horizon, fresh air, avoid rapid head movements.

Illusions & Remedies

Coriolis Effect: Avoid rapid head turns.

Autokinesis/Empty Field Myopia: Move head, scan instruments.

Vertigo: Rely on instruments, ignore conflicting senses.

“SEE/MOVE” Countermeasures

Summarizing the “SEE/MOVE” methods and their applications in resolving aviation-related illusions or sicknesses. It’s important to differentiate them in the exam.

Note: Airsickness is covered in part 1, others will be covered in following parts.

Method

Problem Solved

How It Solves the Problem

Avoid rapid head movement

Coriolis Effect

Prevents conflicting signals in the vestibular system caused by sudden head motions during turns, reducing spatial disorientation.

Move head

Autokinesis

Shifting head position breaks fixation on a stationary light (e.g., a star), confirming it is not actually moving.

Empty Field Myopia

Scanning different areas by moving the head forces the eyes to refocus, counteracting the tendency to focus on a near point in featureless visual conditions.

Dust on Windshield

Adjusting head position changes the viewing angle, revealing that perceived "objects" are dust particles on the windshield.

See natural horizon

Airsickness

Provides a stable external visual reference, reducing sensory conflict between the inner ear and eyes.

Empty Field Myopia

Offers a distant focal point, preventing the eyes from defaulting to a near focus in low-visibility environments.

Autokinesis

Anchors perception to a fixed reference (horizon), negating the illusion of a stationary light "drifting."

Scan all instruments, ignore other senses

Disorientation -> Vertigo

Prioritizes instrument readings over conflicting sensory inputs (e.g., false sensations of climbing or banking), maintaining spatial awareness.
