Part 2: Medical, Drugs & Altitude

Medical and Qualification

1.   Classes of Medical Certificates

Class 1 For: Commercial Pilot License (CPL), Airline Transport Pilot License (ATPL).

Validity: 1 year.

Class 2 For: Private Pilot License (PPL), Recreational Pilot License (RPL).

Validity:

Under 40 years old: 4 years.

Over 40 years old: 2 years.

2.   Loss of Medical Fitness

Temporary Illness:

Class 1: Report if illness lasts > 7 days.

Class 2: Report if illness lasts > 30 days.

Process:

Submit to a DAME (Designated Aviation Medical Examiner).

The DAME will issue a new medical certificate if the pilot is deemed fit.

3.   Disqualifying a Pilot

Physical Disabilities: Poor vision, hearing loss, etc.

Sudden Incapacitation (or Potential): Stroke, heart attack, etc.

4.   CASA Medical Panel

DAME: Designated Aviation Medical Examiner

DAO: Designated Aviation Ophthalmologist

CO: Chief Medical Officer

Medical Specialists: Specialized doctors for specific conditions

Alcohol

1.   Absorption and Effects

Fast Absorption: Directly enters the bloodstream through the stomach and small intestine walls.

Acts as a Depressant:

Causes euphoria/false sense of well-being.

Impairs thinking, judgment, and decision-making.

Destabilizes balance by affecting the inner ear (changes fluid viscosity).

Reduces inhibitions.

2.   Elimination from the Body

10%: Expelled through breath, perspiration, and urine.

90%: Metabolized by the liver.

Metabolism Rate: One standard drink per hour

Right Method: Exercise (increases metabolism).

Wrong Method: Sleep (slows metabolism).

3.   Alcohol Timeline

CASA Requirements:

CAR (Civil Aviation Regulations): No alcohol 8 hours before departure.

BAC (Blood Alcohol Concentration):

Legal limit: 0.02 per 210L of breath before flight.

Even after BAC reaches 0(up to 14h after ingestion), alcohol remains in brain cells for up to 24h, causing hangovers and reduced performance.

Medication

Never take any drug if you intend to fly while the drug is still in your system, unless approved by a DAME.

1.   Non-Prescription/Over-the-Counter Drugs

Generally acceptable, but consult a DAME if in doubt.

2.   Common Drugs and Adverse Effects

Analgesics (Painkillers):

Aspirin (Aspro, Disprin):

Stomach upset.

Excess use: Internal bleeding, kidney impairment.

Codeine (Panadeine):

Addictive, causes constipation.

Paracetamol (Panadol, Dymadon):

Excess use: Liver and kidney damage.

Antihistamines (for colds, flu, sinus):

Don’t use before flight

Adverse effects: Drowsiness, reduced performance.

Ephedrine (nasal spray for hay fever):

Incompatible with blood pressure medications.

Adverse effects: Nasal irritation, prolonged use issues.

Stimulants:

Amphetamines (Benzedrine):

Short temper, restlessness, overconfidence, headaches.

Caffeine:

Sleeplessness, irregular heartbeat.

Antibiotics:

The illness itself may disqualify flying.

Adverse effects: Diarrhea.

Tranquillizers and Sedatives:

Never use without medical consultation.

Side effects: Dizziness, increased susceptibility to alcohol.

Blood Pressure Medication:

Can be used if dosage remains unchanged.

Any dose change requires DAME approval.

Illegal Substances

Cannabis (Marijuana):

Impairs concentration and accuracy.

Effects can persist for 24+ hours.

Amphetamines (Speed, Ecstasy, Benzedrine):

Stimulate the central nervous system ("uppers").

Require depressants ("downers") to sleep.

Disastrous short- and long-term effects.

Opiates (Morphine, Heroin):

Heavy sedatives that quell pain.

Cause psychomotor impairment, reduced mental alertness, and insensitivity to pain (increased risk).

Hyperventilation

1.   Causes and Symptoms

Caused by anxiety or excessive exhalation of carbon dioxide.

Symptoms:

Lightheadedness, dizziness.

Tingling/numbness in extremities (hands, feet).

Sweating, rapid heartbeat (may lead to fainting).

2.   Remedy

Slow down breathing rate.

Decompression Sickness (The Bends)

1.   Causes and Symptoms

Scenario:

Scuba diving: High pressure underwater (30ft depth = 2x sea level pressure) causes nitrogen to dissolve in the blood.

At altitude, nitrogen comes out of solution, causing decompression sickness.

Symptoms:

Stiffness in joints, muscle pain.

Tingling, paralysis.

2.   Remedies

Correct: Descend, use a hyperbaric chamber.

Wrong: Holding breath (can cause air to enter the brain, leading to death).

3.   Risk Altitude

Decompression sickness can occur as low as 8,000ft after scuba diving.

Other Gas Expansion Problem

Gastrointestinal Problems:

Large Bowel: Vented.

Small Bowel: Severe pain.

Worst Foods: Fermented drinks, green vegetables (cabbage, beans).

Imperfect Tooth Fillings:

Can cause severe pain at altitude.

Altitude-Related Numbers to Remember

1.   Atmospheric Pressure and Oxygen Levels

Total Atmospheric Pressure: Halves at 18,000 feet.

Partial Pressure of Oxygen (O₂): Halves at 9,000 feet.

2.   Effects on Smokers

At Sea Level (SL), a smoker’s blood oxygen level is equivalent to a non-smoker’s at 8,000 feet.

At 5,000 feet, a smoker’s blood oxygen level is equivalent to a non-smoker’s at 10,000 feet.

3.   Night Vision Impairment:

Normal people: Impaired above 4,000 feet due to hypoxia.

Smokers: Impaired even at Sea Level.

4.   Supplemental Oxygen Requirements

For Pilots:

Sea Level – 10,000 feet

Not required.

10,000 – 25,000 feet

Continuous flow oxygen.

25,000 – 40,000 feet

Demand oxygen mask (oxygen percentage starts increasing).

At 33,700 feet

Oxygen percentage increases to 100%.

Above 40,000 feet

Pressure demand mask (100% oxygen under pressure).

For Passengers: Supplemental oxygen required above 14,000 feet.
