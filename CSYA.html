CSYA 1 — Power Plants Engines

What you must know

  • Carburettor circuits: idle (bypass), main (venturi ΔP), accelerator pump (transient enrichment).
  • Mixture vs altitude: density ↓ → mixture rich unless leaned.
  • Detonation vs Pre‑ignition: causes, symptoms, consequences.
  • Supercharger (engine‑driven) vs Turbocharger (exhaust‑driven): impeller/diffuser/wastegate types.
  • Rated boost / Critical altitude (full‑throttle height).
  • Handling: avoid over‑boost; smooth throttle; cooldown after high power.

Key rules & definitions

  • Main jet flow: float‑chamber static pressure minus venturi low pressure; avoid over‑enrichment at low IAS.
  • Idling jet bypasses the venturi downstream of closed throttle; transitions off as throttle opens.
  • Accelerator pump prevents lean‑cut on rapid throttle advance.
  • Detonation = explosive end‑gas burn (↑CHT/pressure); drivers: low octane, over‑boost/high MP at low RPM, lean mixture, overheating.
  • Pre‑ignition = ignition before spark (hot spots) → kick‑back/“dieseling”.
  • SC vs Turbo: impeller ↑velocity → diffuser ↑pressure; turbo uses turbine & wastegate to meter exhaust energy.

Exam traps

  • Mixing up detonation (fast burn) vs pre‑ignition (early start).
  • Leaving mixture full‑rich at altitude (over‑rich, power loss, fouling).
  • Assigning wastegates to geared superchargers (they’re turbo hardware).
  • Rapid throttle movements on turbos → over‑boost risk.

Final 60‑second refresh

  • Idle = bypass; Main = venturi ΔP; Accel = transient spurt.
  • Over‑boost + low RPM → detonation risk. Lean with altitude. Cooldown after high power.

Micro‑drills

  • Critical altitude = highest altitude where rated boost/MP can be held at full throttle.
  • Wastegate: open → bypass turbine (low boost); closed → drive turbine (high boost).
  • Two cockpit drivers of detonation you control: mixture/MP/RPM combination; temperature management.

CSYA 2 — Aircraft Systems Systems

What you must know

  • Fuel: aux/boost pump roles, drains (water), pressure/flow indications, check valves.
  • Lubrication: wet‑sump path, bypass logic, oil cooling & temperature control; dip‑stick use.
  • Electrical: battery (Ah), alternator/regulator, over‑voltage relay, bus bars, ammeter/loadmeter, CBs vs fuses, starter/relay, dual mags.
  • Hydraulics: reservoir, pump, accumulator, actuator, check valve; brakes; fluid type; ‘spongy’ with air.
  • Fire detection: thermal switch, thermocouple (rate‑of‑rise), UV/IR; master warning & test.
  • Cooling: fins, baffles, cowl flaps (open for high power/low speed).
  • Retractable gear: up/down‑locks, squat switch, aural/visual warnings, emergency free‑fall/alternate drives.

Key rules & behaviours

  • Fuel drains at low points; water subtler in Jet A (consider chemical test).
  • Oil filter bypass opens if clogged/viscous → unfiltered oil protects against starvation (wear risk).
  • Battery vs alternator: alternator sets higher bus V (14/28 V) and charges battery; low volts pre‑start/post‑shutdown → suspect battery.
  • Loadmeter shows alternator load; ammeter (centre‑zero) shows charge/discharge (negative → alternator offline).
  • CB is protection, not a switch; one reset max if essential.
  • Hydraulic accumulator = “pressure battery” (smooths demand & supplies emergency pressure).
  • Squat switch: prevents gear retraction on ground; gear warning horn with low power + gear not down.

Exam traps

  • Hydraulic fluid is incompressible.
  • Do not keep resetting a hot CB (fire risk).
  • Loadmeter ≠ battery state of charge.
  • Checking oil level immediately after shutdown can mislead (drain‑down delay).

Final 60‑second refresh

  • Oil bypass saves engine from starvation (expect unfiltered oil).
  • Ammeter negative = alternator failed → shed load.
  • Squat switch locks gear on ground; use free‑fall if main system fails.

Micro‑drills

  • Three fire detector types: thermal switch; thermocouple (rate‑of‑rise); UV/IR flame.
  • ‘Spongy’ brakes indicate: air in hydraulic lines (compressible).
  • Why baffles? Seal/direct high‑pressure cowl air through fins for cooling.

CSYA 3 — Instruments Pitot‑static & Gyros

What you must know

  • Airspeeds: IAS, CAS (position/config), EAS (compressibility), TAS (density).
  • Pitot‑static: ASI, ALT, VSI; markings (white/green/yellow arcs; red line).
  • Blockage signatures: pitot vs static; VSI lag; alternate static effects.
  • Gyros: rigidity & precession; air vs electric gyro pros/cons; DI apparent/transport wander; flux‑valve slaving.
  • Compass: construction, fluid damping, dip/pendulous errors, accel/decel errors; hemisphere mnemonics.

Key rules & signatures

  • TAS ↑ with altitude for the same IAS (lower density).
  • Pitot blocked, static OK: ASI behaves like an altimeter (climb ↑, descent ↓).
  • Static blocked: ASI under‑reads in climb/over‑reads in descent; ALT & VSI freeze after equalisation.
  • VSI is laggy; IVSI uses an acceleration pump to reduce lag.
  • DI drift ≈ 15°/hr at poles, ~0 at equator; reset unless flux‑valve slaved.
  • Precession response occurs 90° in direction of rotation.
  • Compass near poles unreliable; lead/lag in turns; accel/decel errors (use mnemonics).

Exam traps

  • ASI is pitot‑static, not gyroscopic.
  • VSI does not show instant ROC/ROD.
  • Alternate static (cabin) changes indications per POH—don’t forget.

Final 60‑second refresh

  • Pitot blocked → ASI ‘altimeters’ behaviour; Static blocked → ALT/VSI freeze; ASI misreads with altitude change.
  • Gyro: rigidity holds plane; precession at 90°. DI drift: reset or use flux‑valve.

Micro‑drills

  • EAS vs CAS: EAS = CAS corrected for compressibility (≈ same <200 kt & <10,000 ft).
  • Two DI error sources (besides friction): apparent (earth rotation) and transport wander.
  • VNE red radial: ‘never exceed’ — structural failure risk.

CSYA 4 — Propellers & CSU Props

What you must know

  • Terms: blade angle, helix angle (pitch), thrust vs torque, BHP vs THP, prop efficiency.
  • Fixed vs variable pitch; ground‑adjustable; CSU governor internals.
  • CSU states: on‑speed, over‑speed (go coarse), under‑speed (go fine).
  • CTM vs ATM; oil‑pressure loss behaviour: single‑engine vs multi‑engine (counterweights).
  • MP vs RPM handling order; over‑boost avoidance.
  • Carb heat effects with CSU; carb‑ice indications FP vs CSU (RPM vs MP drop).

Key rules & behaviours

  • Prop = aerofoil; AoA changes with TAS → fixed pitch is a compromise.
  • BHP (shaft) vs THP (to air); efficiency = THP/BHP (≈50–80%).
  • Governor: flyweights vs speeder spring move pilot valve to meter oil to hub.
  • Over‑speed → blades coarse (↑torque, ↓RPM); Under‑speed → blades fine (↓torque, ↑RPM).
  • Pitch stops: at full‑fine/full‑coarse behaves like fixed pitch.
  • SE oil‑loss → CTM to full‑fine; ME with counterweights/ATM → coarse/feather bias.
  • Power changes: ↑power = RPM→MP; ↓power = MP→RPM (avoid high MP/low RPM).
  • Carb heat: initial power drop, then recovery as ice clears; with CSU watch MP for ice.

Exam traps

  • Oil loss doesn’t always send blades coarse (SE usually goes fine).
  • Using MP like a “thrust lever” without respecting RPM order.
  • At pitch stops, CSU can’t hold RPM constant.

Final 60‑second refresh

  • Over‑speed → add oil → coarse. Under‑speed → dump oil → fine.
  • Power up: RPM→MP. Power down: MP→RPM. Oil loss: SE→fine; ME→coarse/feather bias.

Micro‑drills

  • CTM vs ATM: CTM twists toward fine; ATM toward coarse.
  • Carb‑ice indication: FP → RPM drop; CSU → MP drop (RPM held).
  • Why prop efficiency changes with speed? Blade AoA & advance‑ratio shift.
